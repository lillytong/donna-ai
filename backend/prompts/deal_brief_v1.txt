Read the WHOLE contract once and distil it into a brief covering:
- PARTIES + ROLES: who the parties are and the role each plays in this deal (as the contract
  names them — e.g. the licensor/licensee, supplier/offtaker, the JV partners).
- EACH PARTY'S BUSINESS + INTERESTS: what each party does and what it is getting out of this
  deal, to the extent the contract states or makes it plain.
- THE ECONOMIC SPINE: the core value exchange — what flows to whom, and on what economic terms
  (fees, royalties, pricing, margins, volumes, capital, the consideration that makes the deal
  work). If the money lives in a sibling agreement this contract only references, SAY SO (see
  the scope note below) rather than guessing the numbers.
- KEY COMMERCIAL TERMS + HOW THEY INTERRELATE: the load-bearing terms (term/duration,
  exclusivity, territory, volume/capacity commitments, termination, key conditions) and how
  they depend on or trade off against one another.
- THE DEAL'S PURPOSE: in a sentence or two, what this deal is FOR — the outcome the parties are
  trying to achieve together.

GROUNDING DISCIPLINE — NON-NEGOTIABLE. A wrong brief grounds every later recommendation, so:
- Use ONLY the contract text below. Bring in NO outside knowledge — not about these parties,
  their industry, market norms, typical figures, or anything you happen to know. Nothing that
  is not on the page.
- CITE OR FLAG: when you state a fact, it must be supported by the contract. Refer to where it
  comes from in plain terms (e.g. "the term clause", "the pricing schedule"). Do not invent
  clause numbers.
- When the contract does NOT say something, write "not stated in the contract" rather than
  inventing, assuming, or filling the gap from what would be typical. An honest gap is correct;
  a plausible fabrication is a failure.
- Mark every INFERENCE as an inference ("this implies", "appears to", "likely, though not
  stated") and keep it tightly tied to the text. Never present an inference as a stated fact.
- Do NOT give legal opinions on enforceability or validity. This is a commercial/structural
  brief.

SCOPE — THIS CONTRACT ONLY (v1). You are reading ONE contract. Many deals are split across
sibling documents (an offtake agreement, a JV agreement, a side letter) that you CANNOT see
here. Where this contract clearly depends on terms that live in such a sibling document — most
often the economics — say so honestly: name what is governed elsewhere and that it is outside
this brief, instead of guessing its contents. Flagging an unseen dependency is correct; do not
treat it as a defect to paper over.
